About Me

I am a Ph.D. candidate in Mathematics at the University of North Texas with research interests in applied probability, stochastic processes, and statistical inference. My work asks a simple question: how do past events change the chance of future events?

From financial trades to seismic aftershocks, events often trigger more events. My research focuses on developing new classes of Hawkes processes to better model this self-exciting behavior, particularly when it contains rhythmic or cyclical patterns that classical models miss. My goal is to create frameworks that are theoretically rigorous, computationally efficient, and practically interpretable.

Research

Dissertation: An Oscillatory Kernel for Hawkes Processes

My dissertation introduces an oscillatory, exponentially decaying kernel for Hawkes processes. This model nests the classical exponential case but can also represent periodic or quasi-periodic effects. The primary contributions of this work are:

  • Moment Theory: Developed a toolkit to derive closed-form expressions for the first four moments and a recipe for higher orders.
  • Inference & Asymptotics: Proved consistency and asymptotic normality of the MLE and derived a closed-form approximation of the Fisher Information Matrix for accurate uncertainty estimates.
  • Computation & Validation: Implemented fast simulation and exact likelihood maximization in R/Rcpp, validating the theory with extensive Monte Carlo studies.
  • Applications: Currently applying the framework to real-world data from finance and seismology to demonstrate its predictive power.

Future Research Agenda

  • Multivariate Extensions: Extend the oscillatory kernel to multivariate and network Hawkes processes to model interactions with phase lags.
  • Open-Source Software: Release and maintain an R package for simulation, inference, and diagnostics of the model.
  • Model Generalization: Explore nonparametric and marked Hawkes processes to allow for richer, data-driven oscillatory interactions.

Teaching

Teaching Philosophy: Clarity, Action, and Care

With two decades of teaching experience from Nepal to the University of North Texas, my philosophy is built on three principles. I believe in Clarity to move from intuition to formal understanding, Action to ensure students learn by doing, and Care to create an inclusive community where every student feels seen and supported. My goal is for students to leave my classroom not just with new skills, but with a deeper belief in their own capability to master mathematics.

"After a student scored 54% on a quiz, we created a two-week plan with short practice sessions. By the next quiz, their score rose to 76%, and by the midterm, it was 84%. This student's hard work, combined with targeted support, proved they could master the material."
