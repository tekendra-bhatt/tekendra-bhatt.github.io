Overview

I am in the early stages of building a formal publication record. At present my work is organized around two major projects that share a common theme: begin with rigorous probabilistic theory, design computational tools that reflect that theory, and then check performance on realistic data sets.

The first project develops a likelihood based framework for parametric Hawkes processes, with particular attention to an oscillatory Hawkes model. It covers asymptotic properties of maximum likelihood estimators, Fisher information, and testing for sinusoidal modulation in the excitation kernel. The second project introduces a general differential operator approach to analytic moment formulas for Hawkes processes whose kernels admit a linear differential representation with constant coefficients, covering both monotonic and non monotonic examples.

Project 1 - Likelihood Theory for Oscillatory and General Hawkes Processes

Status: Manuscript in preparation

General asymptotic framework for Hawkes maximum likelihood

This project develops a general framework for maximum likelihood inference in parametric Hawkes processes with nonnegative excitation kernels. I distinguish carefully between a stationary Hawkes process defined on the whole real line and an asymptotically stationary process observed on a finite horizon, and study their associated conditional intensities as functions of the parameter.

Under natural compactness, identifiability, and integrability conditions, I obtain regularity results for these intensities and their parameter derivatives and show that, as the observation window grows, the finite horizon intensity becomes asymptotically equivalent to the stationary intensity uniformly over the parameter space. This leads to laws of large numbers and central limit behavior for the log likelihood and its derivatives and yields consistency and asymptotic normality of the maximum likelihood estimator with the usual Fisher information limit. The assumptions are stated directly in terms of the kernel, so the results apply to a broad class of Hawkes models, not only to the specific oscillatory form considered later.

Oscillatory Hawkes model and Fisher information

Within this general setting I focus on a Hawkes model with a sinusoidally modulated, exponentially decaying excitation kernel. The model has five parameters, including a frequency parameter and an amplitude parameter that control the oscillatory structure. I study the Fisher information matrix for this family, derive closed form expressions in terms of transforms of the kernel, and construct accurate analytic approximations that agree closely with observed information computed from simulated data.

Because the oscillatory parameters are challenging to estimate, I design a multi stage estimation procedure that combines coarse frequency exploration with local likelihood optimization. Simulation experiments show that this protocol can recover the full parameter vector with high precision in realistic sample sizes and delivers reliable standard errors based on the Fisher information.

Testing for sinusoidal modulation

A third component of the project is a test for the presence of sinusoidal modulation in the excitation kernel. In this problem the frequency parameter is a nuisance parameter that is not identified under the null hypothesis of no modulation. Adapting an approach due to Davies, I construct a global score type statistic based on the supremum of frequency specific scores and approximate its distribution using an upcrossing based p value calculation.

Simulation studies indicate that the test has good size and power properties across a range of scenarios. An application to high frequency financial data illustrates how the oscillatory model and the associated test can reveal intraday patterns and regime switching behavior that are not captured by monotone kernels alone.

Project 2 - Analytic Moments for Hawkes Processes via Differential Operators

Status: Technical manuscripts and follow up work in preparation

A general differential operator framework

In this project I develop a general differential operator framework for computing analytical moments of linear Hawkes processes. The idea is to work at the level of cluster factorial moments and to encode the effect of the excitation kernel through a linear differential operator with constant coefficients. This reduces the moment problem to solving a fixed order ordinary differential equation whose structure depends only on the kernel.

The method applies whenever the kernel admits a finite order linear differential representation, which covers a broad class of monotonic and non monotonic kernels. In contrast to some existing approaches for moment calculations in Hawkes processes, the framework avoids heavy machinery and keeps the analysis anchored to a single, carefully chosen differential operator.

Applications to exponential, gamma, and oscillatory kernels

I first validate the framework on the classical exponential kernel, where it recovers known moment formulas. I then extend the analysis to gamma type kernels, and obtain closed form expressions for the mean event count and a structured route toward higher order moments. This highlights how the same operator based viewpoint can accommodate kernels with richer temporal structure.

The method is then applied to the sinusoidally modulated, exponentially decaying kernel that appears in my oscillatory Hawkes model. In this setting the associated operator naturally reflects the oscillatory behavior of the kernel, and the resulting moment expressions capture both decay and oscillation in a transparent way. The framework provides consistent treatment of exponential, gamma, and oscillatory cases within a single analytic toolkit.

Scope and significance

The main contribution of this project is to show that many Hawkes kernels used in applications, including both monotonic and non monotonic examples, can be handled by the same differential operator approach. Compared with classical approaches using infinitesimal generators, martingale methods, or the multi-step expansion techniques, the proposed method avoids heavy stochastic machinery and replaces it with a single, systematically constructed ODE. This yields a generic pathway to analytical or semi analytical moment expressions that is conceptually simple, flexible, and well suited for further work on dependence structure, model comparison, and diagnostic tools for Hawkes processes.

Looking Ahead

Together these projects aim to provide a coherent toolkit for Hawkes processes that combines rigorous asymptotic theory, analytic formulas for dependence structure, and carefully designed computational methods. My goal is to develop these results into a sequence of research articles that are accessible to applied probabilists and practitioners, and that support student projects at both the undergraduate and graduate level.
