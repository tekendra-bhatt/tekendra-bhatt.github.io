Overview

I am in the process of building a formal publication record. At present my work is organized around two major projects that share a common theme: start from rigorous probabilistic theory, develop efficient computational tools, and then validate methods on realistic data sets.

The first project develops a complete likelihood based framework for an oscillatory Hawkes process, including asymptotic theory, Fisher information, and testing for sinusoidal modulation. The second project develops a general differential operator approach to analytic moment formulas for Hawkes processes with kernels that can be represented by linear differential equations with constant coefficients.

Project 1 - Likelihood Theory for Oscillatory Hawkes Processes

Status: Manuscript in preparation

Model and setting

This project studies a class of Hawkes processes with a sinusoidally modulated, exponentially decaying excitation kernel. The model includes both monotone and non monotone kernels as special cases and is driven by a constant baseline intensity together with a kernel that satisfies an appropriate stability condition. The same assumptions cover processes started at time zero and stationary processes that conceptually start at negative infinity.

Rigorous asymptotic theory for maximum likelihood

The first contribution is a rigorous treatment of consistency and asymptotic normality of maximum likelihood estimators for this family of Hawkes models. A key step is to control the conditional intensity for both processes that start at time zero and stationary processes that start at negative infinity, and to show that their conditional intensities are asymptotically equivalent to that of the stationary Hawkes process with the same kernel.

I establish boundedness and asymptotic equivalence of these intensities under structural assumptions on the kernel that apply to an entire class of kernels, not only to the specific oscillating kernel I propose. This class can include monotone and non monotone kernels as long as they satisfy a stability criterion. These results then feed into a uniform law of large numbers for the log likelihood and the derivation of asymptotic normality of the estimator.

Fisher information and estimation challenges

The second contribution is a detailed study of Fisher information and parameter estimation. I derive a closed form expression for the Fisher information matrix using Laplace transforms, together with an approximate analytic Fisher information for the proposed oscillatory kernel. I then compare these analytic matrices with the observed Fisher information obtained from simulated data and show that the approximations are very accurate in realistic regimes.

The model has five parameters. In particular, the frequency parameter and the parameter that governs the amplitude of oscillations are difficult to estimate in practice. I therefore design a multi stage estimation protocol that combines grid searches, profile likelihood, and local optimization. Simulations show that this procedure recovers all parameters with high precision and provides useful standard errors based on the Fisher information.

Testing for sinusoidal modulation

The third contribution is a test for the presence of sinusoidal modulation in the excitation kernel. In this setting the frequency parameter is a nuisance parameter that is not identified under the null hypothesis of no modulation. Following the approach of Davies, I construct a global score test based on the supremum of per frequency score statistics. The p value is approximated using the upcrossing method for a chi squared process with one degree of freedom.

Through simulation studies I verify that the proposed test has good control of the type I error rate and useful power in relevant parameter regimes. An application to high frequency financial data illustrates how the method can reveal intraday, regime switching market dynamics that are not visible under a purely monotone excitation kernel.

Project 2 - Analytic Moments for Hawkes Processes via Differential Operators

Status: Technical manuscripts and follow up work in preparation

Sinusoidally modulated exponential kernel

The first part of this project presents a complete analytical framework for calculating the first four moments of a linear Hawkes process with a sinusoidally modulated, exponentially decaying kernel. Starting from the branching process representation, I derive a Volterra integral equation for the cluster probability generating function. By repeatedly differentiating the generating function, I obtain a recursive system of Volterra equations for functions related to factorial moments of cluster sizes.

I then introduce a differential operator method that converts this system of integral equations into a recursive system of third order linear ordinary differential equations with constant coefficients. I prove that the corresponding characteristic equation has one real root and a pair of complex conjugate roots for all physically relevant parameter values. Finally, I give a detailed, step by step solution strategy for these equations, including an explicit derivation of the particular solution for the second order case and a complete pathway to the analytic moment formulas.

Erlang-2 kernel and generalization

The second part of the project applies this differential operator framework to a Hawkes process with an Erlang 2 excitation kernel. Starting from the recursive Volterra equations for derivatives of the cluster generating function, I identify a minimal annihilating differential operator for the Erlang 2 kernel and compute the associated convolution transform operator.

This leads to a second order ordinary differential operator that governs all factorial moment densities. I solve the resulting ordinary differential equation for the first moment density explicitly and obtain a closed form expression for the expected event count. I then outline the construction of the forcing term for the second moment density and the corresponding particular solution, making clear the role of resonance when the forcing shares exponents with the homogeneous solution.

The main idea is not tied to these specific kernels. The method can be applied to any Hawkes process whose kernel can be represented by a differential equation with constant coefficients. This creates a unified and systematic route to analytic or semi analytic moment formulas for a broad class of Hawkes models.

Looking Ahead

Together these projects aim to provide a coherent toolkit for Hawkes processes that combines rigorous asymptotic theory, analytic formulas for dependence structure, and carefully designed computational methods. My goal is to develop these results into a sequence of research articles that are accessible to applied probabilists and practitioners, and that support student projects at both the undergraduate and graduate level.

A brief summary of my broader research interests is available on the Research page, and my curriculum vitae lists talks, manuscripts in progress, and related work.
