Overview

I am in the process of building a formal publication record. At present my work is organized around two major projects that share a common theme: start from rigorous probabilistic theory, develop efficient computational tools, and then validate methods on realistic data sets.

The first project develops a complete likelihood based framework for an oscillatory Hawkes process, including asymptotic theory, Fisher information, and testing for sinusoidal modulation. The second project develops a general differential operator approach to analytic moment formulas for Hawkes processes with kernels that can be represented by linear differential equations with constant coefficients.

Project 1 - Likelihood Theory for Oscillatory Hawkes Processes

Status: Manuscript in preparation

General framework for Hawkes maximum likelihood

This project begins with a general study of consistency and asymptotic normality of maximum likelihood estimators for parametric Hawkes processes with a nonnegative excitation kernel g(t; θg) 1{t ≥ 0}. The analysis explicitly distinguishes the stationary Hawkes process N*t, defined on the whole real line, and the asymptotically stationary process Nt, defined on [0, ∞), with associated intensities λ*t,θ and λt,θ.

Under compactness, identifiability, and integrability assumptions (A1 to A4), I establish uniform Lp bounds for the intensities and for their first and second order derivatives with respect to the parameters. A key result is an asymptotic equivalence statement supθ∈Θ ∥λt,θ − λ*t,θ∥p → 0 as t → ∞, with analogous convergence for the derivatives. The proofs combine Hölder, Jensen, and Minkowski inequalities with martingale bounds for compensated counting measures and kernel dominated integrability conditions.

I further show mixing and ergodicity of the stationary intensity process λ*t for bounded test functions and extend ergodicity to classes of functions with polynomial growth under moment conditions. This yields time average laws of large numbers that hold uniformly over the parameter space Θ. These ingredients provide exactly the regularity needed for maximum likelihood consistency and a quadratic expansion of the log likelihood leading to asymptotic normality with the usual Fisher information limit. Importantly, this framework is not restricted to a single kernel. It applies to any nonnegative kernel that satisfies the regularity conditions, so it covers a broad class of monotone and non monotone excitation functions.

Model and oscillatory setting

Within this general framework I focus on a family of Hawkes processes with a sinusoidally modulated, exponentially decaying excitation kernel. The model includes both monotone and non monotone kernels as special cases and is driven by a constant baseline intensity together with a kernel that satisfies an appropriate stability condition. The same assumptions cover processes started at time zero and stationary processes that conceptually start at negative infinity.

Fisher information and estimation challenges

A second major component of the project is a detailed study of Fisher information and parameter estimation for this oscillatory Hawkes family. I derive a closed form expression for the Fisher information matrix using Laplace transforms, together with an approximate analytic Fisher information for the proposed oscillating kernel. I then compare these analytic matrices with the observed Fisher information obtained from simulated data and show that the approximations are very accurate in realistic regimes.

The model has five parameters. In particular, the frequency parameter and the parameter that controls the amplitude of oscillations are difficult to estimate in practice. I therefore design a multi stage estimation protocol that combines coarse searches over frequency, profile likelihood, and local optimization. Simulations show that this procedure recovers all parameters with high precision and provides useful standard errors based on the Fisher information.

Testing for sinusoidal modulation

The third contribution is a test for the presence of sinusoidal modulation in the excitation kernel. In this setting the frequency parameter is a nuisance parameter that is not identified under the null hypothesis of no modulation. Following the approach of Davies, I construct a global score test based on the supremum of per frequency score statistics. The p value is approximated using the upcrossing method for a chi squared process with one degree of freedom.

Through simulation studies I verify that the proposed test has good control of the type I error rate and useful power in relevant parameter regimes. An application to high frequency financial data illustrates how the method can reveal intraday, regime switching market dynamics that are not visible under a purely monotone excitation kernel.

Project 2 — A Differential-Operator Method for Analytical Moments of Hawkes Processes

Status: Technical report and manuscript in preparation

A general method based on differential operators

This project develops a unified analytical framework for computing closed-form moments of linear Hawkes processes by transforming the usual recursive Volterra system for cluster factorial moment densities into a system of ordinary differential equations with constant coefficients. The method begins from the branching-process representation of a Hawkes cluster and the associated probability generating function (PGF). By repeatedly differentiating the PGF at zero, one obtains a hierarchy of coupled Volterra integral equations for the factorial moments. The core contribution is the identification of a minimal annihilating differential operator that eliminates the kernel from these convolution equations, converting each Volterra equation into a linear ODE of fixed order whose coefficients depend only on the kernel.

This differential-operator reduction provides a general and tractable method applicable whenever the excitation kernel admits a finite-order linear differential equation with constant coefficients. This includes a large class of monotonic and non-monotonic kernels, far beyond the standard exponential family. Compared with classical approaches using infinitesimal generators, martingale methods, or the multi-step expansion techniques of Cui, Hawkes, and Yi :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}, the proposed method avoids heavy stochastic machinery and replaces it with a single, systematically constructed ODE.

Applications to exponential, gamma, and sinusoidally modulated kernels

For the exponential kernel the method reproduces known closed-form moments, confirming correctness and serving as the base case in the hierarchy. For the gamma kernel the annihilating operator becomes second-order, leading to a recursive pair of ODEs for the first two factorial moment densities. Solving these yields explicit formulas for the mean event count and a transparent pathway toward higher-order moments. This provides a more direct alternative to PGF-based or generator-based derivations in the literature.

The method handles kernels with oscillation or modulation in a natural way. For the proposed sinusoidally modulated, exponentially decaying kernel—non-monotonic by construction—the annihilating differential operator becomes third-order, with one real and a complex conjugate pair of characteristic roots. This structure controls the form of both the homogeneous and particular solutions. I give a full derivation of the first four moments, including an explicit formula for the forcing term that appears in the second-moment equation and a careful treatment of resonance when the forcing shares exponents with the homogeneous solution. The resulting moment formulas capture the oscillatory behavior of the model in closed form.

Extension to kernels with higher-order structure (Erlang-2 and beyond)

The same differential-operator framework applies to excitation kernels that correspond to higher-order linear ODEs. I illustrate this by working out the Erlang-2 kernel, identifying the appropriate annihilating operator, computing the convolution transform, and deriving the ODE governing the first factorial moment. This leads to a closed form for the mean count E[NT] and provides a clear recipe for constructing the forcing term in the second-moment equation. The structure of the derivation shows how resonance and root multiplicity influence the particular solution.

General significance

Overall this project introduces a generic, algebraically driven method for obtaining analytical moments of Hawkes processes across a broad class of both monotonic and non-monotonic kernels. Its strength lies in reducing a potentially complicated probabilistic problem to a single linear differential equation with constant coefficients, making the approach simple to implement and extensible to many kernel families used in applications. The framework complements and simplifies existing methods and provides the analytic foundation needed for later developments in likelihood theory and model comparison.

Looking Ahead

Together these projects aim to provide a coherent toolkit for Hawkes processes that combines rigorous asymptotic theory, analytic formulas for dependence structure, and carefully designed computational methods. My goal is to develop these results into a sequence of research articles that are accessible to applied probabilists and practitioners, and that support student projects at both the undergraduate and graduate level.

A brief summary of my broader research interests is available on the Research page, and my curriculum vitae lists talks, manuscripts in progress, and related work.
