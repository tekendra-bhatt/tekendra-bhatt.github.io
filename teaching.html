Teaching Philosophy

My teaching is guided by four principles: articulation, engagement, inclusion, and growth. They shape how I design courses, run class meetings, and work with students in both in person and fully online formats.

  • Articulation. New ideas are introduced through approachable questions and examples before we move to formal definitions and proofs. Each class begins with a short overview and ends with a summary so students see how pieces fit together.
  • Engagement. Students learn mathematics by doing mathematics. I use think–pair–share, small group work at the board, and targeted practice so that students regularly explain ideas, compare strategies, and receive feedback.
  • Inclusion. I aim to build classrooms where it is safe to ask questions and admit confusion. I invite participation in multiple ways: speaking in class, writing, online discussion, polls, and one–to–one meetings.
  • Growth. Assessment is organized to encourage improvement rather than one–time judgment. Frequent low–stakes checks, clear rubrics, and opportunities to correct mistakes help students see progress and adjust their study strategies.

The modality changes tools, not goals. In person courses emphasize board work and group problem sessions; online courses use structured modules, discussion forums, and carefully proctored assessments, while keeping the same emphasis on clarity and interaction.

Classroom Practices

  • Structured course design. Weekly modules with checklists, goals, and resources help students organize their work and reduce uncertainty about expectations.
  • Active learning. Class time is regularly used for guided problem solving, small group discussions, and board work where students compare reasoning and strategies.
  • Retrieval and spaced practice. Short warm–up problems, mixed review questions, and cumulative quizzes encourage regular review of important ideas.
  • Early and proactive support. I monitor participation and early assessments and reach out to students who may be falling behind, helping them connect with office hours and other resources.
  • Purposeful technology. Tools such as Canvas, WebAssign, Desmos, and GeoGebra are used to support conceptual understanding and feedback, not to overwhelm students with platforms.

Mentoring and Student Projects

My research on Hawkes processes and related stochastic models provides a natural pipeline for student projects that moves from rigorous theory, to computation, to real or realistically simulated data.

  • Introductory and intermediate projects. Students simulate Poisson and self–exciting processes, visualize clustering, and fit simple models to time-stamped data using R, gaining intuition about randomness in time.
  • Advanced undergraduate projects. Students work with extended Hawkes models, compare different excitation kernels on data, and interpret parameter estimates in the context of an application area such as finance, reliability, or environmental events.
  • Graduate projects. Students take on focused pieces of the theory or computation, such as proving specific limit properties for a class of kernels, analyzing efficiency of an estimator, or implementing and benchmarking new algorithms for simulation or maximum likelihood.

In each case I try to connect the level of mathematical rigor to the student’s background while keeping the full story visible: from theoretical formulation, to code, to interpretation in a real world setting.
