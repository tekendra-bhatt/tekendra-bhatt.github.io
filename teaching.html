Teaching Philosophy

My teaching is guided by four principles: articulation, engagement, inclusion, and growth. They shape how I design courses, run class meetings, and work with students in both in person and online settings.

  • Articulation. New ideas are introduced through approachable questions and examples before we move to formal definitions and proofs. Each class begins with a short overview and ends with a summary so students see how pieces fit together.
  • Engagement. Students learn mathematics by doing mathematics. I use think pair share, small group work at the board, and targeted practice so that students regularly explain ideas, compare strategies, and receive feedback.
  • Inclusion. I aim to build classrooms where it is safe to ask questions and admit confusion. I invite participation in multiple ways: speaking in class, writing, online discussion, polls, and one on one meetings.
  • Growth. Assessment is organized to encourage improvement rather than one time judgment. Frequent low stakes checks, clear rubrics, and opportunities to correct mistakes help students see progress and adjust their study strategies.

Whether I am teaching face to face, fully online, or in a hybrid format, these principles remain the same. The modality changes the tools, not the underlying goals of clarity, activity, and support.

Classroom Practices

  • Structured course design. Weekly modules with checklists, goals, and resources help students organize their work and reduce uncertainty about expectations.
  • Active learning. Class time is regularly used for guided problem solving, small group discussions, and board work where students compare reasoning and strategies.
  • Retrieval and spaced practice. Short warm up problems, mixed review questions, and cumulative quizzes encourage regular review of important ideas.
  • Early and proactive support. I monitor participation and early assessments and reach out to students who may be falling behind, helping them connect with office hours and other resources.
  • Technology with a purpose. Tools such as Canvas, WebAssign, Desmos, and GeoGebra are used to support conceptual understanding and feedback, not to overwhelm students with platforms.

Mentoring and Student Projects

My research on Hawkes processes and related stochastic models provides a natural pipeline for student projects. The typical pattern is: rigorous probabilistic formulation, computational exploration, and then application to real or realistically simulated data.

  • Introductory and intermediate projects. Students simulate Poisson and self exciting processes, visualize clustering, and fit simple models to time stamped data sets using R, gaining intuition about randomness in time.
  • Advanced undergraduate projects. Students work with extended Hawkes models, compare different kernels on data, and interpret parameter estimates in the context of an application area such as finance or reliability.
  • Graduate projects. Students take on focused pieces of the theory or computation, such as proving specific limit properties for a family of kernels, studying efficiency of an estimator, or implementing and benchmarking new algorithms for simulation or maximum likelihood.

In each case I aim to connect the level of mathematical rigor to the student’s background while keeping the full story visible: from theory, to code, to real world interpretation.
