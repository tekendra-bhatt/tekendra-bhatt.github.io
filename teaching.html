Teaching Philosophy

My teaching is guided by four principles: articulation, engagement, inclusion, and growth. Together they shape how I design courses, run class meetings, and interact with students.

  • Articulation. Concepts are introduced through clear questions, simple examples, and intuitive explanations before moving to formal language and proofs. Each class session begins with a short overview of goals and ends with a brief recap so students see the structure of the course.
  • Engagement. Students learn mathematics by doing it. I use think pair share, small group board work, and targeted practice problems so that students regularly explain ideas to peers, not just listen to lectures.
  • Inclusion. I work to create an environment where it is safe to ask questions and admit confusion. I encourage multiple forms of participation, such as speaking in class, writing responses, using discussion boards, or visiting office hours.
  • Growth. Assessment is designed to support improvement. I rely on frequent low stakes quizzes or checks, transparent rubrics, and feedback that separates conceptual understanding from algebraic or computational slips.

Classroom Practices

  • Structured course shells. Each course uses a clear weekly structure with checklists, learning targets, and organized resources so students always know what to work on next.
  • Active problem sessions. I dedicate regular time to guided problem solving where students work in small groups, share solutions on the board, and critique different approaches.
  • Retrieval and spaced practice. Short warm up problems at the beginning of class and mixed review questions encourage students to revisit key concepts regularly.
  • Early outreach. I monitor participation and early assessments to identify students who may be struggling, then follow up with individualized emails and suggestions for office hours and study strategies.

Mentoring and Undergraduate Projects

I design project ideas that let undergraduates experience aspects of research while staying closely connected to course content. Examples include:

  • Simulation projects that compare Poisson processes, classical Hawkes models, and oscillatory versions on synthetic data.
  • Data projects where students analyze real time stamped data and fit simple event models, interpreting parameters in the context of the application.
  • Small reading and presentation projects in real analysis or probability that build skill in mathematical communication.

These experiences prepare students for internships, further study, and mathematically intensive careers while integrating naturally into the curriculum.
